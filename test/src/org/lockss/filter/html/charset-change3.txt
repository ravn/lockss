Management School, Huazhong University of Science and Technology, Wuhan, Hubei, 430074, China E-mail: xuehai0225@126.com E-mail: hwf2403@163.com E-mail: Geder2020@163.com *Corresponding author
Abstract: This study investigates the relationship between capital structure and the private benefits of control of listed Chinese firms. A sample of firms listed on the Chinese stock markets is used to estimate the private benefits of control. The private benefits of control are approximately 18% (on average) in China. There is a statistically significant negative relationship between Short-Term Debt (STD) and the private benefits of control. In addition, a positive relationship exists between Long-Term Debt (LTD) and the private benefits of control. Finally, a statistically significant negative relationship exists between total debt and the private benefits of control. Keywords: capital structure; private benefits of control; minority stockholder protection; Chinese firms. Reference to this paper should be made as follows: Zhaoguo, Z., Weifeng, H. and Jing, C. Biographical notes: Zhang Zhaoguo is a Professor of Accounting and Finance at the Huazhong University of Science and Technology (HUST) in Wuhan, China. His research interests are property rights theory, corporate governance and corporate finance His research has been published in journals such as Accounting Research, Management World, and China Soft Science. The main focus of the literature on investor protection and its role in the development of financial markets has, thus, been on the amount of private benefits that controlling shareholders extract from the firms that they run (La Porta et al., 2000). Because the private benefits of control can distort the financial policies of a firm, researchers have examined the significance of the roles played by various factors (such as corporate ownership structure, legal foundations, and extra-legal institutions) on the private benefits of control and the protection of minority shareholders. Such structures as pyramids, cross-holdings, and dual-class shares can be used to separate ownership from control, thus increasing the potential for private benefits of control (Bebchuk et al., 2000). The literature discusses how better legal protection and stronger social norms can improve the protection of minority shareholders against expropriation and reduce the private benefits of control (La Porta et al., 2000; Dyck and Zingales, 2004). However, there is little in the literature about the effect of capital structure on private benefits of control. Jensen and Meckling (1976) argue that the interest payments associated with debt can constrain the expropriation of funds from dispersed shareholders by imposing fixed obligations on corporate cash flow. This argument was further developed by Jensen (1986, 1989), who argued that the debt associated with leveraged buy-outs reduces the amount of free cash flow that could otherwise be used for unprofitable investments. Lang et al. (1996) demonstrate that debt curtails investment by firms with poor prospects, and that leverage increases when growth opportunities are diminished. Maloney et al. (1993) show that leverage improves managerial decision-making on acquisitions. Mehran (1992) and Berger et al. (1997) provide empirical evidence that managers who wish to avoid active monitoring or value-linked incentive schemes tend to decrease leverage. Despite these studies, there is little in the literature regarding the role of debt in curbing the private benefits of control. It is apparent from this brief introduction that gaps exist in the literature regarding the role of capital structure and debt in curbing the private benefits of control. The present study, therefore, examines how capital structure affects the private benefits of control, with particular emphasis on the relationship between debt and the private benefits of control in Chinese firm. There are several reasons for choosing China as a setting for the study of these matters. Firstly, China is in a state of transition from a command system to a market economy. Unlike developed economies, corporate governance in China is in the early stage of development and has many of the features of weak shareholder protection that typically exist in underdeveloped markets. The share proportion of the largest shareholders in listed Chinese firms is (on average) 44%, managers and boards of directors are usually nominated by controlling shareholders, and takeover events are rare. The protection of the minority shareholders and the enhancement of corporate governance are pressing issues in such an environment. Secondly, the private benefits of control in Chinese firms are relatively high. The price for control transactions is higher than the price for non-control transactions; moreover, the price for control transactions is also higher than the average international price (Tang and Jiang, 2002; Shi, 2003; Ye, 2003; Deng and Zeng, 2004). The curbing of the private benefits of control is, thus, of importance for listed Chinese firms. Thirdly, Chinese firms are heavily dependent upon STD for financing and company law is ambiguous about the rights of debt-holders.

Theoretical framework
2.1 Capital structure and private benefits of control
In general, the private benefits of control can be said to exist when an incongruity exists between the benefits actually accrued from a controlling shareholding and the benefits that should accrue in terms of the voting rights represented by that shareholding. As Dyck and Zingales (2004) note, the private benefits of control are not shared among all the shareholders in proportion to the shares owned; rather, they are enjoyed exclusively by the party in control. Private benefits of control can be divided into two categories: • • pecuniary non-pecuniary.
The pecuniary benefits are the more visible but not necessarily the more important means by which corporate resources can be used by controlling shareholders or senior executives (Jensen and Meckling, 1976). This is because personal pecuniary benefits – such as managerial salaries and perquisites (perks) – are usually subject to scrutiny and control. The non-pecuniary benefits are less overt; they include: • • • the prestige and social status that come with ownership the power to decide on a business strategy independence from superiors.
These benefits are less amenable to scrutiny and control, and if a company has a single controlling shareholder such non-pecuniary benefits can be especially significant. In an analysis of the agency problems that can arise between professional managers and dispersed corporate shareholders, Jensen and Meckling (1976) argue that the interest payments associated with debt constrain managerial expropriation of funds from dispersed shareholders by imposing fixed obligations on corporate cash flow. Other authors also note that interest payment obligations curtail managerial entrenchment and enhance managerial performance (Grossman and Hart, 1982; Stulz, 1990; Harris and Raviv, 1990; Maloney et al., 1993). This argument is further developed by Jensen (1986, 1989), who argue that the debt associated with leveraged buyouts forces managers to channel their corporation’s free cash flow appropriately. In this situation, debt financing has the potential to improve information transmission, reduce information asymmetry, and restrain insiders’ rent-seeking behaviour.
2.2 Measuring private benefits of control
By their very nature, private benefits are unobservable and can be estimated only indirectly. In general, the literature suggests two methods of measuring private benefits: • • the block premium approach the price difference between two classes of stock.
The first approach was pioneered by Barclay and Holderness (1989), who argue that the market price is determined by non-controlling shareholders who are basically uninterested in the private benefits of control. According to the block premium approach, the difference between the price per share paid by the acquirer and the price quoted on the market the day after the sale’s announcement represents an estimate of private benefits of control enjoyed by the controlling shareholder. Using this methodology, Barclay and Holderness (1989) report that the average premium paid in US block trades is 20%. Using a similar approach, Dyck and Zingales (2004) conduct an international comparison of private benefits of control by studying 393 controlling block sales in 39 countries. They find that, on average, the private benefits of control represented a premium of 14% (with a very wide range from –4% to +65%). The second method of estimating the value of private benefits of control uses the price difference between two classes of stock with similar or identical dividend rights, but different voting rights. An acquirer of voting stocks purchases them at a price that includes the value of the private benefits accruing to the controlling party. Thus, the market price of the voting stocks reflects the expected value of the private benefits, whereas the market price of the non-voting stocks does not. With appropriate assumptions on the probability of a control contest, it is possible to determine the value of private benefits from the market price differential between the voting stocks and the non-voting stocks (Kang and Kim, 2006). Some studies on this method have focused on dual-class firms that pay identical dividends on both classes of shares (Levy, 1982; Lease et al., 1983; Megginson, 1990). Other studies have considered dual-class firms with systematic dividend differentials (Zingales, 1994; Smith and Amoako-Adu, 1995). In China, there are tradable stocks and non-tradable stocks, but the proportion of non-tradable stocks is much larger than that of tradable stocks. Because the non-tradable stocks are mainly transferred through private negotiation, and because there are no stocks with differential voting rights, any estimation of the private benefits of control is significantly more difficult than is the case in other financial environments. It is apparent that the methodologies described above are not applicable to China without adjustment. Given these difficulties, Tang and Jiang (2002) use the differential between the transfer price for large blocks of stock and the equity per share to measure the private benefits of the controlling shareholder. This approach takes the ownership characteristics of listed Chinese firms into consideration and provides an illustration of how empirical studies in this field might be undertaken. However, their methodology did not make a comparison with the difference between the price paid for non-control transactions and the equity per share. Thus, the differential between the transfer price for large blocks of stock and the equity per share cannot reflect the degree of expropriation of the controlling shareholder. Ye (2003) makes progress in this respect. He uses the difference in price paid for control transactions and non-control transactions of non-tradable stocks to measure the private benefits of the controlling shareholder. Data and methodology
The sample for the present study consisted of A-share Chinese firms listed on the Shenzhen Stock Exchange market and the Shanghai Stock Exchange market. The analysis considered both the control and non-control transactions for non-tradable stocks of these firms in each year between 1997 and 2004, both inclusive. In total, the sample is consisted of 102 listed firms, involving 112 pairs of observations and 224 sets of transactions. The statistics come from three sources: • • • the websites of the Shenzhen stock exchange and the shanghai stock exchange the control transfer announcements of listed firms by China security a database provided by Sinofin Information Services and GTA Information Technology Co. Ltd.
The following measures were adopted in this study. Capital structure. In general, capital structure refers to the ratio of total debt to total assets (TDA). This debt includes STD and LTD. As previously noted, STD has a shorter reimbursement period, is more sensitive to asset liquidity, and has a prior claim over other debts. Such debt places firms under pressure with regard to repayment of principal, payment of interest, and contract renegotiation. The concept of capital structure in the present study, therefore, also includes the ratio of STD to LTD. Size of firm. Because larger firms are more closely monitored by supervisory agencies and institutional investors (Barclay and Holderness, 1989; Leleux et al., 1997), this pressure might have a significant influence on reducing the block-holders’ power to extract private benefits of control. To control for the effect of firm size, the present study used the log value of total book assets. Growth rate. According to the trade-off theory, firms holding future growth opportunities tend to borrow less because growth opportunities cannot be collateralised. Moreover, agency theory argues that firms have a tendency to expropriate wealth from debt-holders (Myers, 1977; Jensen, 1986). Firms with greater growth opportunities have more flexibility to invest sub-optimally, and thus expropriate wealth from debt-holders to shareholders because of the asset-substitution effect. Ongoing growth opportunities imply a conflict between debt and equity interests. To control the effect of growth opportunity, the present study used the growth rate of net income.

The average ratio of STD to total assets STD was 39.71% (median 38.37%; range 11.31–100%). The value 0.3837 indicates that approximately 38% of total assets were represented by STD, which attests to the fact that Chinese firms are heavily dependent on STD for financing (with resulting high operating risks). One reason for this is the underdevelopment of the Chinese LTD market. Another reason is the difficulty of accessing long-term credit from financial institutions for Chinese firms. The average ratio of LTD to total assets was 5.61% (median 2.22%; range 0–78.69%). The mean total debt to total capital ratio (TDA) was 44.55% (median 44.54%; range 0.81–99.64%). This shows that about 45% of total assets were financed by debt capital. This compares with a mean total debt level in the G7 countries of 66% in 1991 (Rajan and Zingales, 1995) and 51% in developing countries (Booth et al., 2001). The 44.55% total debt level in China is, thus, less than that of most developed countries, but the difference is unremarkable. However, the difference between total debt and LTD ratio was much more pronounced in China. The LTD level was only 5.61% in China compared with a mean of 41% in the G7 countries and 22% in developing countries. The substantially lower figure for LTD in China reflects the fact that the listed Chinese firms are mainly financed by share capital, rather than by debt. Bank loans provide short-term financing for working capital, but equity is the main source of finance for capital investment. In summary, the descriptive statistics in Table 1 suggest that listed Chinese firms have higher STD ratios and lower LTD ratios than firms in other countries.
4.2 Regression results
To gain an insight into the relationship between capital structure variables and the private benefits of control, variables were introduced into the model one at a time. Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) regression results are presented in Table 2. The results from the regression models (1), (2), and (3) show that the independent variables explained the private benefits of control determinations of the firms at 65.32%, 46.52%, and 69.52%, respectively. The F-statistics confirm the validity of the estimated models, and the coefficients are statistically significant at a level of confidence of 99%. The OLS estimation in the first column of Table 2 shows a coefficient of –0.518 at the 1% significance level; this indicates that there was a statistically significant negative relationship between STD and the private benefits of control. This suggests that STD can constrain the private benefits of control for controlling shareholders in listed Chinese firms; conversely, it suggests that increasing short-term debt will lead to a decrease in the private benefits of control. As shown in the second column in Table 2, there is a statistically significant positive association between LTD and the private benefits of control. This implies that an increase in the LTD position is associated with an increase in the private benefits of control. This can be explained by the fact that most LTD comes from banks, which are mostly controlled by government; the bank-firm relationship does not fully comply with a market system in China. Moreover, company law is ambiguous about debt-holders’ rights and is seriously flawed in allowing shareholders and government agencies too much power in bankruptcy procedures. Under these circumstances, LTD restraint on listed firms can be very weak, and employing a high proportion of LTD could increase the private benefits of control.

5
Conclusion
The private benefits of control stem from an incongruity between the control rights and the cash-flow rights of the controlling shareholder. Such benefits are enjoyed exclusively by shareholders who hold control, but not by all shareholders. Jensen and Meckling (1976) argue that debt constrains managerial expropriation by imposing fixed obligations on corporate cash flow. If so, there is a negative relationship between debt and the private benefits of control. The present study has evaluated this proposed relationship between capital structure and the private benefits of control using a sample of listed Chinese firms during an 8-year period (1997–2004). The difference between control and non-control transactions for non-tradable stocks in the same year was used as a proxy for the private benefits of control. The study finds that the private benefits of control is 18.52% on average, which is close to that of emerging market countries but higher than that of countries with a developed market economy. The results reveal that a statistically significant negative relationship exists between STD and the private benefits of control, which suggests that short-term debt helps to constrain the private benefits of control of listed firms in China.
